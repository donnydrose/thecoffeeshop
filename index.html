The Dishwasher

The dishwasher was usually the last one to clock out, but we were never too far apart from being among the last to leave the restaurant. We often nodded to each other from across the subway tracks. There were so many unspoken conversations when we exchanged racks of dirty and clean glasses during service hours, but it was only while I waited for the train at dawn that I tried to imagine his life before he came to this corner bar at 16th Street and Union Square.

1

I often wondered if his village was like mine. I imagined all the things that were familiar to him—the cock-a-doodle-doo of the rooster at the rising of a star that peeked through the wooden cracks of his flat house, the smell of mashed plantains, and the heavy dust of the village road.

2

The graveyard shift as a dishwasher at The Coffee Shop was Owia’s third job. Dressed in white overalls, he started and ended his shifts at the sink. He would scrape the remains of food off the plates and into the full garbage and rock back and forth humming a lullaby with the swish swash of the water in the deep silver sink.

Poets In The Page

by Tim Pourbaix

My memories of home are like broken chords. I have faint memories now of waking up to the morning mist over rice paddies and the dew drops on the lotus leaves—beads of water like gentle eyes looking up at me. I miss those unassuming eyes, watery reflections of a life in slow motion. Here, the days are fast and harsh, and I get lost in my stories of a life I can barely recall. I no longer know which parts of me are real. I am a slippery dream.

I have so many versions of myself. One version began at the legendary Coffee Shop, where I got my start as a bartender, where I felt a sense of belonging with a group of people who were mostly far from home in so many ways.

Andrew's Song

by Andrew

I recall feeling like a bottom feeder yet special because I had some window into this city that only some of us were given access to; dodging sidewalk vomit at the darkest hour when the garbage trucks hauled up to 16th street, screeching a slow and long noise, I felt special knowing that we were innately different from the others. I used to imagine the people in the nearby buildings: stockbrokers, schoolteachers, bank tellers, and receptionists hiding their heads under their pillows for one last snooze as I walked into the subway, into the underground, away from the moon’s faded silver face.

Who I Am

by Hosanna Littlebird

“A who you, a who you, a who you.”

I was four or five years old when I wiped my tears and tried to respond with my name, but the words flew off from inside of me out to another realm.

The bird whisked me away to sit on a dark cloud. I was told a story about suffering and how one day it will be my wings. The bird promised to return one day to take me back up to the dark cloud for just a second to see the moment when I had lost my voice.

I’m California dreaming now. Crows and cooper hawks with black stripes on their tails sing to me in the open desert. I’m on the other side, writing New York in LA. I no longer go to bars to drink alone. I only walk by at closing hour to hear the “last call” bar song Hotel California through slightly opened doors.
